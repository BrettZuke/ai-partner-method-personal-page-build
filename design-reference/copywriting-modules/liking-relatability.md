# Copywriting — Liking-Relatability (The 11 Variables of Likeness)
*Source: Copy Elite — Copywriting 101, Liking-Relatability module*

---

## The Core Idea

People buy from people/brands they like and trust. If your audience doesn't like and trust you, it doesn't matter how good your copy or sales message is — they won't buy.

Sales = transference of belief over a bridge of trust.

Marketers get obsessed with funnels and selling tactics and forget the most obvious question: **does our audience even like and trust us?**

---

## The 11 Variables of Likeness

### 1. Relatability / Similarity
We like people we relate to — people who share our feelings (internal) and our traits (external).

The more your audience believes you FEEL how they FEEL, LIKE the things they LIKE, and TALK the way they TALK... the more they'll like and trust you.

**How to use it:** Use their language. Talk about their specific conditions. Reference shared experiences, interests, hatreds, culture, background.

### 2. Familiarity / Consistency
We like things we're familiar with. Familiarity feels safe. This is the "mere exposure effect" — the more we're exposed to something, the more we like it.

Consistency creates predictable familiarity. Same appearance, same energy, same values = people feel they know and can trust you, even if they don't actually know you.

**How to use it:** Show up consistently. Same voice, same energy, same style. Predictability = safety = trust.

### 3. Authenticity / Humanity
We like people who feel real. Not performing. Not trying too hard. Not hiding something.

We also like people who show their imperfections — it makes them human and relatable. We naturally distrust "perfect" people.

**How to use it:** Share real failures (if relatable to audience). Use your actual voice, not corporate speak. Laugh at yourself. Admit what you don't know.

**Warning:** Authentic does NOT mean oversharing. Being authentic is choosing to share *certain* moments, not everything. There's attractiveness in mystery.

### 4. Happiness / Warmth
We prefer happy over sad. We like people who make us feel happy, safe, and accepted. Warm people are "easy" to be around.

People forget what you said but remember how you made them feel.

**How to use it:** Don't take everything too seriously. Show gratitude, excitement, joy. Leave people feeling better than you found them.

### 5. Competence with Humility
We like people who are successful at what they do — competence attracts and signals safety.

But competence quickly becomes repulsive when paired with arrogance. Humility isn't thinking less of yourself — it's focusing on others. We're drawn to people who are "quietly powerful." Their results speak for themselves without needing to announce it.

**How to use it:** "How I" not "How to." Show results, don't brag about them. Let social proof do the talking.

### 6. Humor / Storytelling
We like and remember people who make us feel something — especially happiness (humor) and engagement (stories). Both trigger dopamine.

Humor makes you likable. Stories make you memorable.

**How to use it:** Weave humor and stories into copy naturally. Don't force it. A well-placed joke or a specific story beats a generic claim every time.

### 7. Reciprocity / Creativity
We like and feel like we "owe" people who give us something first. Giving value without immediately asking for something back builds goodwill and trust.

Creativity amplifies reciprocity — when you put real thought into what you give, people feel more compelled to return the favor.

**How to use it:** Lead with genuine free value. Make it useful, thoughtful, or unexpected. The more creative and unique, the stronger the reciprocity.

### 8. Attractiveness / Confidence
We're drawn to attractive and confident people. Physical attractiveness is a cheat code for influence — but every other variable in this list also contributes to overall "attractiveness."

Confidence ties to the SAFE emotion. We want to be led by people who are confident in themselves. (But remember: arrogance is not confidence.)

**How to use it:** Write with confidence. Show up looking decent. Don't be apologetic or wishy-washy in your messaging.

### 9. Skill / Social Proof
We like people who are extremely skilled — even in things we don't care about. Skill is scarce, and we ascribe value to scarce things.

Social proof is the psychological shortcut where we rely on what other people do/like instead of thinking critically ourselves. If a million people follow someone, our brain assumes they're worth following.

**How to use it:** Demonstrate skill through your work, not by claiming it. Use real numbers, testimonials, results as social proof. Let evidence do the convincing.

### 10. Anti-Beneficiary Bias (Non-Neediness)
We like people who don't seem like they *need* to be liked. We're less trusting of people who have more to gain from an exchange than we do.

Non-neediness signals confidence and competence. It makes the exchange feel safer.

**How to use it:** Write as if you don't care whether they take the action or not. Never beg. Never pressure. The less you need them, the more they trust you.

### 11. The Cult Leader 5
Five psychological levers that explain the most influential movements in history:

1. **Encourage Their Dreams** — Most people were told to "be reasonable" and give up on big dreams. When you tell them they CAN achieve them, they'll follow you.
2. **Justify Their Failures** — People want to hear it's "not their fault." Redirect blame to something external and they'll trust you.
3. **Allay Their Fears** — We hate feeling scared. If you make people feel safe — even without solving the problem — they'll want to be around you.
4. **Confirm Their Suspicions** — We love being right. When you confirm something they already suspected, they feel "attached" to you.
5. **Throw Rocks At Their Enemies** — The enemy of our enemy is our friend. Attack something they already hate and you're instantly on the same side.

## Leveraging Likeness in Copy (The 3-Step Process)

Once you know WHAT emotion to push (subtext), you need to decide HOW to push it (text). The 11 Variables of Likeness are the "how."

**Step 1:** Know your audience's specific conditions (from the Conditions Deck)
**Step 2:** Choose the subtext — which emotion(s) you want to push
**Step 3:** Choose the text — test different ways to push that emotion using the 11 Variables

The secret is picking the way to push the emotion that creates the most likeness/trust with YOUR specific audience.

**The "Price Is Right" Framework:** Every word carries its own emotion and connotation. Your job is to find the perfect words for your audience — close enough to resonate but not so extreme they feel fake.

## Before/After Examples

**Relatability** (Pain Bullet): "Your life sucks" → "You survived school, but the 9-5 grind is killing you from the inside out. You can't imagine doing this for another year. You feel completely replaceable."

**Familiarity** (Offer Headline): "Sign 3-5 Business Owners Using Our Proven Acquisition System" → "Sign 3-5 Copywriting Clients Using Our Proven Outreach System"

**Authenticity** (Allaying Fears): "If that sounds like you, don't worry." → "If that's you rn... I've got your back brother."

**Humor** (Easy emotion): "This system is so easy, even a monkey could do it!" → "We built this system with one goal in mind: Make it so easy a monkey could sign a client. (And my lil brother got a client using so that means we're pretty close LOL.)"

**Confidence** (Pushing Big): "This could be a big opportunity for you." → "This opportunity will change your life FOREVER. I say this with 100% certainty because it radically changed my life."
