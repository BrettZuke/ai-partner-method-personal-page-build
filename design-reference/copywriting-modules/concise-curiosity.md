# Copywriting — Concise-Curiosity
*Source: Copy Elite — Copywriting 101, Concise-Curiosity module*

---

## The C.U.B. Method

The three ways copy fails:

**Confusing** — The reader doesn't understand what you're saying. They stop reading.

**Unbelievable** — The reader understands but doesn't believe it. They dismiss it.

**Boring** — The reader understands and believes it, but doesn't care. They tune out.

Every editing pass should be a C.U.B. check: Is any part of this confusing, unbelievable, or boring?

---

## The 7-Step Editing Process

1. **Read it out loud** — If it sounds unnatural, it'll read unnatural. Fix it.
2. **Cut everything that doesn't serve a purpose** — No filler words, no redundant phrases, no padding.
3. **Simplify language** — Replace complex words with simple ones. Never use a $10 word when a $1 word works.
4. **Fix confusing sections** — Ask: "Would a 10-year-old understand this?" If not, rewrite it.
5. **Fix unbelievable claims** — Every bold claim needs proof, logic, or a reason why.
6. **Fix boring sections** — Inject specificity, curiosity, or an emotional angle.
7. **Final CUB check** — Read through one more time looking for anything confusing, unbelievable, or boring.

## Fixing Confusing Copy

Confusion kills momentum. Readers won't work to understand you — they'll just leave.

**Causes of confusion:**
- Jargon or technical language your audience doesn't use
- Vague, abstract statements with no concrete meaning
- Long, complex sentences trying to say too much at once
- Unclear pronoun references ("it", "they", "this" without clear anchors)
- Jumping between ideas without transitions

**How to fix it:**
- Use your audience's exact language (from research, comments, DMs, reviews)
- Replace abstractions with specific examples: "a lot of money" → "$10k/month"
- Break long sentences into two shorter ones
- Read it as if you know nothing about the topic — where do you get lost?

## Fixing Unbelievable Copy

If they don't believe you, they won't buy. Skepticism is the default state of most readers.

**What makes copy unbelievable:**
- Claims that sound too good to be true with no evidence
- Generic, vague results ("changed my life," "massive success")
- No specificity — specificity = credibility
- Sounding like every other marketer

**How to fix it:**
- **Specificity:** "Lost 20 lbs in 6 weeks" beats "lost a ton of weight"
- **Proof:** Testimonials, screenshots, case studies, before/afters
- **Logic:** Give the mechanism — *why* does this work? Walk them through the reasoning.
- **Authority:** Credentials, experience, results, endorsements
- **Risk reversal:** Guarantees that remove the fear of being wrong

---

## Fixing Boring Copy

Boring copy gets skimmed or abandoned. Even if it's clear and believable, no one acts on content they don't care about.

**What makes copy boring:**
- Generic statements that apply to everyone and no one
- No emotional stakes — no pain, no dream, no tension
- Predictable structure — reader knows what's coming next
- Flat, corporate tone with no personality
- Too much explanation, not enough storytelling

**How to fix it:**
- Lead with the most interesting thing, not the most logical
- Use specificity to make it vivid and real
- Add contrast, tension, or surprise
- Inject personality — write like a human, not a press release
- Use pattern interrupts: unexpected comparisons, bold statements, provocative questions

## Curiosity (The Knowledge Gap)

Curiosity is created when there's a gap between what someone knows and what they want to know.

**The formula:** Tease the existence of information → Withhold just enough → Make them feel they NEED to know it

Curiosity keeps people reading. Without it, even great copy gets abandoned.

**Two rules:**
1. Open the loop (tease the information)
2. Don't close it too early (make them work through your content to get the answer)

---

## 16 Ways to Create Curiosity

1. **Ask a question** — "What does every successful coach do that struggling ones don't?"
2. **Make a counterintuitive statement** — "The worst thing you can do after a good workout is..."
3. **Tease a list** — "3 things killing your conversion rate (most people never catch #2)"
4. **Use "the reason why"** — "The reason most people never hit six figures has nothing to do with their skills."
5. **Name something without explaining it** — "There's a concept I call the Invisible Ceiling — it's why smart, hardworking people plateau."
6. **Make a bold claim** — "This one shift is responsible for 80% of the results my clients get."
7. **Hint at a secret or insider knowledge** — "Most coaches won't tell you this, but..."
8. **Use specificity as bait** — "Exactly 4 words I say on every sales call that changed everything."
9. **Create contrast** — "Everyone told me to post more. I posted less. My following tripled."
10. **Use the word "this"** — "This is why your emails aren't getting opened."
11. **Promise a reveal** — "By the end of this, you'll understand exactly why..."
12. **Reference something relatable then flip it** — "You've probably been told X your whole life. It's wrong."
13. **Incomplete loop in a headline** — Subject line or hook that can only be resolved by reading further
14. **Numbered specificity** — "The 3-word phrase that ended a 6-month sales slump"
15. **Call out a false belief** — "You don't have a traffic problem. You have a trust problem."
16. **Use "most people"** — Creates tribal contrast. "Most people do X. Here's what actually works."

## Holding Attention

Once you have attention, you have to hold it. Two variables determine this:

### Variable 1: Hook Strength
The hook must create enough curiosity/pain/desire to justify continuing. A weak hook = people drop off immediately.

Hook types that work:
- Question that creates a knowledge gap
- Bold or counterintuitive claim
- Vivid specific scenario they recognize
- Direct call-out of their pain or identity

### Variable 2: Information Release Pace
Drip information strategically. Give enough to satisfy the current micro-curiosity, but always open the next loop before closing the last one.

**The linked chain:** Each sentence/paragraph creates a micro-curiosity that pulls them to the next one. Never answer everything at once. Never leave them with nothing to wonder about.

**Techniques:**
- End paragraphs on a curiosity hook, not a conclusion
- Use transition phrases that tease what's coming: "Here's where it gets interesting..." / "But that's not the whole story..."
- Vary sentence length — short punchy sentences create urgency, longer ones slow things down for emphasis
- Use line breaks strategically to create visual momentum

## Concise-Curiosity Checklist

Run every piece of copy through this before sending:

**Clarity (Anti-Confusion)**
- [ ] Would someone who knows nothing about this understand it?
- [ ] Are there any vague or abstract statements that need a concrete example?
- [ ] Is every sentence necessary, or is something just padding?

**Credibility (Anti-Unbelievable)**
- [ ] Does every bold claim have support (proof, logic, specificity)?
- [ ] Are results specific enough to be believable?
- [ ] Is there any statement that sounds like generic marketing fluff?

**Engagement (Anti-Boring)**
- [ ] Does the hook create genuine curiosity or emotional pull?
- [ ] Is there a knowledge gap that makes them want to keep reading?
- [ ] Are there any sections where a reader might check out?
- [ ] Is there personality and specificity throughout, not just at the top?

**Attention Retention**
- [ ] Does each section create a micro-curiosity that pulls to the next?
- [ ] Are loops opened and closed at the right pace?
- [ ] Does the ending deliver on the promise of the hook?
